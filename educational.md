## MODULE 1 — Introduction to Life and Biological Principles
**Description:** Explore what defines life, how organisms are classified, and the foundational principles that guide biological science.

---

### LESSON 1: Characteristics of Living Organisms
**Description:** Learn what makes something “alive” and understand the universal features shared by all living organisms.

#### SECTION 1: Defining Life
**Title:** Defining Life  
**Description:** Understand the set of characteristics that distinguish living organisms from non-living matter.  
**Main Content:**  
Life is defined by a series of biological characteristics that collectively separate living organisms from non-living entities. These include organization, metabolism, homeostasis, growth, adaptation, response to stimuli, and reproduction. All living organisms, from microscopic bacteria to complex mammals, follow these principles in one form or another.  
Living organisms are highly organized, meaning their structures and systems are arranged in an orderly hierarchy—molecules form organelles, organelles form cells, cells form tissues, and so on. This structural organization enables the precise regulation needed for survival.  
Another essential trait is metabolism—the sum of all chemical reactions within a living organism. These reactions allow organisms to extract energy from nutrients, build new molecules, repair damaged tissues, and maintain internal balance.  
Homeostasis is the ability to maintain internal stability despite changes in the external environment. Whether it's regulating body temperature, pH levels, or water balance, organisms constantly adjust their internal processes to survive.  
Living things also grow and develop, responding to genetic instructions. They adapt over generations through natural selection, and they respond to stimuli in real time. Finally, all living organisms reproduce, ensuring the continuation of their species.  

---

#### SECTION 2: Levels of Biological Organization
**Title:** Levels of Biological Organization  
**Description:** Explore the hierarchy of biological structures, from molecules to ecosystems.  
**Main Content:**  
Biological organization follows a structured hierarchy that helps scientists study life at different scales. At the smallest level, atoms combine to form molecules, which build the complex biological compounds essential for life, such as DNA, proteins, and carbohydrates.  
These molecules form organelles—specialized structures within cells responsible for tasks such as energy production, protein synthesis, and waste management. Cells represent the fundamental unit of life. Some organisms consist of a single cell (unicellular), while others are composed of trillions of cells working together (multicellular).  
Cells form tissues that perform specialized functions, such as muscle tissue or nervous tissue. Multiple tissues work together as organs, and organs form organ systems, like the circulatory or respiratory system.  
At a larger scale, individual organisms interact with others of their species to form populations. Several populations living together in the same environment create a community. When communities interact with abiotic (non-living) factors such as soil, water, and climate, they form ecosystems.  
Understanding how these levels connect provides insight into how life functions at every scale—from microscopic cells to the global biosphere that encompasses all life on Earth.

### LESSON 2: Classification of Living Organisms
**Description:** Learn how scientists categorize life and why biological classification is essential.

#### SECTION 1: The Five/Three Domain System
**Title:** The Five/Three Domain System  
**Description:** Explore how life is grouped into biological domains and kingdoms.  
**Main Content:**  
Biologists classify organisms into hierarchical categories based on shared traits, genetic similarities, and evolutionary history. The most widely accepted modern system includes three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya.  
Bacteria and Archaea consist of prokaryotic organisms—unicellular life forms lacking a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Despite both being prokaryotes, they differ significantly in genetic sequences, membrane composition, and biochemical pathways. Archaea often thrive in extreme environments such as volcanic vents, salt lakes, or acidic springs, earning them the name “extremophiles.”  
The domain Eukarya includes all organisms with complex cells: animals, plants, fungi, and protists. These organisms possess a nucleus and specialized organelles, enabling complex behaviors, multicellularity, and diverse metabolic strategies.  
Within domains, organisms are further classified into kingdoms, phyla, classes, orders, families, genera, and species. This hierarchical structure helps scientists communicate about organisms, understand evolutionary relationships, and predict shared traits between species.  

---

#### SECTION 2: Binomial Nomenclature
**Title:** Binomial Nomenclature  
**Description:** Learn how scientists name species using a universal two-part naming system.  
**Main Content:**  
Binomial nomenclature is a standardized system for naming organisms developed by Carl Linnaeus. Each organism receives a two-part Latin name: the genus (capitalized) and the species (lowercase). For example, *Homo sapiens* refers specifically to modern humans.  
This system reduces confusion that arises from common names, which may vary across languages and regions. Scientific names are recognized globally, ensuring consistency in communication.  
The genus represents a broader category that includes species with similar characteristics. The species name identifies the unique organism within that genus. This classification system also reflects evolutionary relationships—species in the same genus share a common ancestor.  
Binomial nomenclature allows biologists to organize life in a logical, meaningful way, connecting observational biology with evolutionary theory and modern genetics.

## MODULE 2 — Cell Biology
**Description:** Explore the structure, function, and processes of cells, the fundamental units of life.

---

### LESSON 1: Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells
**Description:** Learn the major differences between simple and complex cells.

#### SECTION 1: Prokaryotic Cells
**Title:** Prokaryotic Cells  
**Description:** Understand the structure and simplicity of prokaryotic cells.  
**Main Content:**  
Prokaryotic cells are the simplest and most ancient forms of life, found primarily within the Bacteria and Archaea domains. They lack membrane-bound organelles and a true nucleus. Instead, their DNA is located in a nucleoid region.  
Prokaryotes have a rigid cell wall that provides structure and protection. Many possess additional features such as flagella for movement, pili for attachment, and capsules that help them evade immune responses.  
Despite their simplicity, prokaryotes exhibit incredible diversity in metabolic pathways. Some harness energy from sunlight (photosynthetic bacteria), while others extract energy from chemical reactions involving sulfur, nitrogen, or iron compounds.  
Prokaryotes reproduce rapidly through binary fission and adapt quickly to environmental changes. Their simplicity allows them to thrive in virtually every environment on Earth, making them essential to nutrient cycling, digestion, and ecological balance.

---

#### SECTION 2: Eukaryotic Cells
**Title:** Eukaryotic Cells  
**Description:** Explore complex cells with specialized structures and compartments.  
**Main Content:**  
Eukaryotic cells are larger, more complex, and highly organized. They possess a true nucleus that houses DNA and multiple membrane-bound organelles that perform distinct functions.  
Organelles include mitochondria (energy production), the endoplasmic reticulum (protein and lipid synthesis), the Golgi apparatus (processing and packaging molecules), and lysosomes (waste digestion). Plant cells additionally contain chloroplasts for photosynthesis and large central vacuoles for storage.  
The presence of organelles enables compartmentalization—separating different biochemical processes to increase efficiency. This structural sophistication supports multicellularity, allowing cells to specialize into tissues and organs.  
Eukaryotic cells communicate using chemical signals, enabling coordination in complex organisms. This communication regulates growth, immune responses, reproduction, and behavior.

### LESSON 2: Cell Processes and Energy
**Description:** Understand how cells obtain energy and perform essential life functions.

#### SECTION 1: Cellular Respiration
**Title:** Cellular Respiration  
**Description:** Learn how cells convert nutrients into usable energy.  
**Main Content:**  
Cellular respiration is the process by which cells extract energy from glucose to produce ATP, the molecule that powers nearly all cellular activities. The process occurs in three main stages: glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain.  
Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm and breaks glucose into pyruvate while producing small amounts of ATP. The pyruvate then enters the mitochondria, where the Krebs cycle generates electron carriers that feed into the electron transport chain.  
In the final stage, electrons pass through a series of protein complexes in the mitochondrial membrane, producing a large amount of ATP. Oxygen acts as the final electron acceptor, making aerobic respiration far more efficient than anaerobic pathways.  
This energy supports essential biological processes such as muscle contraction, protein synthesis, and cell division. Without cellular respiration, life as we know it could not exist.

---

#### SECTION 2: Photosynthesis
**Title:** Photosynthesis  
**Description:** Explore how plants convert sunlight into chemical energy.  
**Main Content:**  
Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria transform light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. This process occurs mainly in chloroplasts within plant cells.  
Photosynthesis consists of two major stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent Calvin cycle. In the light-dependent stage, chlorophyll molecules absorb sunlight, energizing electrons that drive the production of ATP and NADPH. Water molecules are split, releasing oxygen as a byproduct.  
During the Calvin cycle, ATP and NADPH are used to convert carbon dioxide into glucose. This stage does not require light and occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast.  
Photosynthesis is essential for life on Earth. It produces oxygen and forms the base of nearly all food chains. The sugars generated by photosynthesis provide the energy that fuels ecosystems and sustains life across the planet.
